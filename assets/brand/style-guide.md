# TakeoffMonkey — Social Content Style Guide

A reference for drafting LinkedIn + Instagram posts (and the weekly newsletter) for TakeoffMonkey. Hand this to Claude (or any assistant) before writing a post so the output sounds like Tommy and lands with the right people.

---

## 1. The Objective

TakeoffMonkey teaches commercial sitework contractors how to use everyday tech and AI to run a tighter, more profitable shop — especially around **estimating, takeoff, and project management**.

Every post has ONE job: **solve one small, real inconvenience the reader feels this week.** Lead with the pain. Show the fix. Be honest about the limits. That's it. Posts build trust now so the brand earns the bigger work later (the soft tie-in: AI speeds the grunt work, a human expert still owns the number).

Each post also drives one action: **subscribe to the weekly newsletter** ("the week's tips, fully baked out with links").

---

## 2. The Audience

**Who:** Owners and estimators at commercial sitework contractors — landscape, irrigation, hardscape, concrete. Roughly **$5M–$250M in revenue.**

**What they're like:**
- Smart, experienced operators. They run real businesses and know their trade cold.
- **Tech-overwhelmed, NOT dumb.** Many still run on paper, spreadsheets, and gut feel — not because they can't learn, but because nobody's ever shown them in their language.
- Busy, practical, skeptical of hype and salespeople. They've been burned by software that promised the world.
- They live in a world of bid day, takeoffs, scope creep, change orders, RFIs, plan revisions, GCs, and architects.

**The golden rule:** Talk to them like a peer on the jobsite, never down to them. They just haven't been shown — so show them.

---

## 3. The Voice (TK_Standards_2026)

Write like **a smart foreman who happens to know tech** — not a software salesman, not a tech blogger.

- **Plain-spoken, confident, jobsite-direct.** Say it straight.
- **Short sentences.** Punchy. One idea at a time.
- **Concrete examples from their world.** Not "improve workflow efficiency" — say "the GC asks how wide the pad is and your tape's in the truck."
- **No jargon.** If a 55-year-old grading foreman wouldn't say it, don't write it. No "leverage," "synergy," "seamless," "robust," "game-changer."
- **No guilt, no shaming.** Never imply they're behind or doing it wrong. Frame it as a smarter move, not a fix for failure.
- **Honest about limits.** Always name what the tool CAN'T do. This is what builds trust and separates TakeoffMonkey from every hype peddler.

**Sign-off (always):** `— Tommy, TakeoffMonkey`

**Quick gut-check before publishing:** Does this solve ONE small, real inconvenience a sitework contractor feels this week? If not, rework it.

---

## 4. Post Structure

### LinkedIn
1. **Hook line** — lead with the pain, a relatable jobsite moment. (e.g. "You're standing on a graded lot and the GC asks, 'Roughly how wide is that pad?'")
2. **Short teaching body** — 2–3 concrete steps the reader can actually follow. Name the real tool. Include real, verified links.
3. **An honest limitation** — what it's good for vs. what it's NOT for.
4. **One engagement question** — invite a reply. (e.g. "What's the one measurement you always end up eyeballing on a walk?")
5. **3–4 hashtags** — pick from `#Sitework #ContractorTech #Estimating #Construction`.
6. **Newsletter CTA** — "The week's tips, fully baked out with links — subscribe to the TakeoffMonkey newsletter at [SUBSCRIBE LINK]."
7. **Sign-off** — "— Tommy, TakeoffMonkey"

### Instagram
- Tighter, punchier, scroll-stopping. Same lesson, less words.
- Scannable lines (emoji bullets are fine here — 📱 🤖 📏 etc.).
- The win in a few lines, then the honest limit in one line.
- **1–2 hashtags + 🍌** (the banana is the brand mascot cue).
- Newsletter CTA, then sign-off "— Tommy, TakeoffMonkey".

### Tone difference between the two
LinkedIn = teach a peer over coffee. Instagram = grab them mid-scroll and give them the win fast.

## 5. Tool Spotlights (the meatier format)

Roughly once a week, a post goes deeper on ONE specific, real, named tool. A spotlight must:
- Name the tool and the ONE pain it kills.
- Give 2–3 concrete sitework uses (estimating / PM / purchasing).
- Include an **honest cost + limitation note** (real pricing tier, platform availability).
- Link to the tool's **real, current website** — verified, never invented.
- End with the newsletter CTA, signed "— Tommy, TakeoffMonkey".
- Title format: `Tool Spotlight: <Tool Name>`.

---

## 6. Accuracy Rules (non-negotiable)

- **Always web-search to verify** any tool name, price, feature, platform, or URL before writing. Never invent a link or a feature.
- Give honest **pros AND cons.** A spotlight with no limitation reads like an ad.
- For anything that depends on current state-of-the-art (especially AI topics), refresh with a quick search — don't write from memory.
- When the real newsletter signup link isn't available yet, use the placeholder `[SUBSCRIBE LINK]` and flag it.

## 7. Brand Reference (TK_Standards_2026)

- **Colors:** Banana `#F5ED60`, Jungle Green `#00391F`, Concrete Gray `#F1F2F2`, Rich Black `#231F20`.
- **Font:** Montserrat.
- **Logo:** White reversed logo on a photo is an approved lockup. Keep clear space around it. Never stretch, rotate, skew, or recolor it.
- **Images:** Text-free, photoreal sitework scenes (a contractor on a graded lot, plans on a tablet at a truck tailgate, etc.) with the white logo composited bottom-right. Never ask an image model to render text — AI mangles it; text gets added later in Canva.

## 8. Voice Examples (from real posts)

**Good hook (pain-first):**
> "You're standing on a graded lot and the GC asks, 'Roughly how wide is that pad?' Your tape's in the truck. The truck's parked 200 feet back."

**Good honest-limit line:**
> "This is AR off your camera, not a laser. Fine for 'about 40 foot,' not fine for a cut list or a bid quantity."

**Good reframe (no guilt):**
> "Your phone already has a tape measure built in. Most guys never open it."

**Good IG punch:**
> "Your phone is already a tape measure. 📏 GC asks how wide the pad is. Tape's in the truck. Don't eyeball it —"

> Talk to a smart, busy sitework contractor like a foreman who knows tech. Solve one real annoyance, show the exact steps with real links, be honest about the limits, and point them to the newsletter. Plain words. Short sentences. No hype, no guilt. — Tommy, TakeoffMonkey.
